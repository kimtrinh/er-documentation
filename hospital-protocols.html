STEMI Patients — Receiving Centers & Protocol

🏥 Clerk Workflow
  1. CLERK FAXES EKG ASAP, waits for confirmation. F/U with phone call to St. B's ER to notify that EKG has been faxed to them. Once we receive fax confirmation:
  2. CLERK CALLS St. B's ER physician & transfers call to our ER Dr. to discuss. (make sure you have St. B's ER Dr. name)
  3. If our Dr. indicates to ACTIVATE A "STEMI":
    CLERK DIALS "911":
    "I'm calling from Kaiser Fontana Emergency Room. We have a patient that needs to be transported to St. Bernadine's for a STEMI."

    AMR will ask: our address 9961 Sierra Ave., ER — Pod ___ Bed ___; name of ordering Dr. (our ER physician); name of receiving Dr. (St. B ER physician).
  4. What goes with patient? Copy of EKG (faxed) · Face Sheet (clerk prints) · patient chart (RN prints). Put together in lg. white AMR transport envelope.
  5. Give our RN the "STEMI" form to complete (located in QI Tools binder on clerk's desk).
🎯 Goals — Patient arriving non-EMS at Kaiser Ontario or Fontana ED
  1. Door-in to door-out <30 minutes
  2. EKG within 10 minutes
  3. Determination of eligibility for STEMI protocol
  4. Establish that the patient wants invasive treatment
    • Consider potential injury to kidneys
    • Need to rescind any existing DNR standing order
  5. Consider road conditions for transfer (traffic · weather · availability of transport)
  6. Is the patient stable for transport?

Code 24 / Code Stroke — BEFAST
Emergency Medicine · Neurology · TeleNeurology · KP Fontana & Ontario
🧠 Code Stroke
Time is brain. Use the BEFAST screen for any patient with possible stroke symptoms. Code 24 extends the thrombectomy window for select late-presenting patients (LSN 6–24 h with NIHSS ≥ 6 and confirmed intracranial occlusion).
🅱️ BEFAST — Signs of Stroke
B
Balance changes
E
Eyes — vision changes
F
Face — facial droop
A
Arms / Legs — weakness
S
Speech — slurred
T
Time — don't stall; call
📝 Remember to use the .EDHelp Smartphrase.
🛤️ Two Pathways
Last Seen Normal 0–6 hours · or Wake-Up Stroke
→ Call CODE STROKE
LSN 6–24 hours · NIHSS ≥ 6
  1. Page TeleNeurologist 323-699-4444 to discuss
  2. If discussion yields Code 24: activate Code 24 & order Code24 CTH + Code24 CTA
  3. If CTA reveals intracranial occlusion → call Code Stroke
🕒 Code 24 Process Summary
  1. Triage — Patient arrives with BEFAST symptoms, last-seen-normal between 6–24 hours.
  2. Physician Assessment — BEFAST symptoms, LSN 6–24 h, NIHSS ≥ 6.
  3. Physician Calls Code 24 — Page TeleNeurologist at 323-699-4444; discuss case. If candidate, ED physician activates "Code 24" and orders "Code 24 CTH" + "Code 24 CTA".
  4. Radiological Review — Local radiology reviews imaging. If intracranial occlusion is identified, ED physician calls a Code Stroke.
  5. Call Code Stroke — For evaluation and set-up of transfer; TeleNeurologist responds to the Code Stroke.
  6. Prepare Transfer — Use the ETAP form to expedite transfer. ETAP will call the TeleNeurologist for further set-up. Nurse must ensure the patient's chart is closed the moment the patient leaves the hospital doors so the time the patient left is captured accurately.
  7. Transfer to CSC for further treatment.

IV Versed — ED RN Scope Clarification
Emergency Medicine · Nursing Scope · FAQ
✅ Within Scope
Bottom line
IV Versed (midazolam) is within the scope for ED RNs. This kills a recurring floor-level misunderstanding that IV Versed requires an ICU/procedural-sedation-trained nurse.
The one carve-out
  • Contingent workers (travelers / registry / non-competency-signed-off nurses) cannot perform procedural sedation because they are not comp'd for it.
  • That restriction applies to the procedural sedation role specifically — not to routine IV Versed administration for anxiolysis/seizure/etc.
Source
Mike Schwartzwald (Chief, ED) · Feb 19, 2026 · internal clarification email

Fluorescein Strip Replacement — OMC Drops
Ontario Medical Center · ED Operations · Ophthalmic Supplies
💧 Ops Note
What changed
OMC is replacing fluorescein strips with Altafluor-Benox ophthalmic drops. FMC is mirroring.
Key workflow points
  • Order the drops in HealthConnect — not the strips — so RNs can pull from Pyxis.
  • Drops must be stored in the refrigerator.
  • Each bottle is single-use only — cannot be shared between patients.
  • Altafluor-Benox = fluorescein + benoxinate (topical anesthetic combo).
Source
Chris Sanine / OMC pharmacy · Mar 19, 2026 · internal ops email

Pediatric Multi-System Inflammatory Syndrome (MIS-C)
Pediatrics · Emergency Medicine · PTAC
🔴 Critical Peds
Consider in any pediatric patient with prolonged fevers, Kawasaki-like features (conjunctivitis, rash, peripheral edema), and GI symptoms (diarrhea, abdominal pain).
⚠️ Cardiogenic Shock Pearls
  • May be hypoxic; if not perfusing, will need a pressor (milrinone or epinephrine — both can run peripherally).
  • In CHF / pulmonary edema — will not respond to IV fluids (will get worse).
  • Antecubital line preferred; a hand 24g is acceptable to begin resuscitation.
🧪 Recommended Workup (Peds Rheum)
CBC/diff · Lytes · BUN · Creatinine · Glucose · LFTs · Albumin · Bagged UA · CRP · ESR · Ferritin · Fibrinogen · D-dimer · Triglycerides · LDH · Troponin · BNP · IL-2 receptor
📞 Disposition
  • Call PTAC immediately for transfer.
  • If unstable, ask to be connected to the Peds Intensivist on call.
  • Request robot evaluation in the ED to assist with resuscitation; PICU attending can guide pressor management via video.
Priapism — Phenylephrine Preparation
Emergency Medicine · Urology · Procedural
🟠 Procedure
Medication Prep — Phenylephrine 1 mg/mL
  1. Using a 3 mL syringe + 18g needle, withdraw and discard 1 mL from a 10 mL vial of 0.9% NaCl.
  2. With the same syringe, withdraw 1 mL of 1% phenylephrine (10 mg/mL) and add it to the remaining 9 mL of NaCl. Final concentration: phenylephrine 1 mg/mL.
  3. Using an insulin syringe, draw up 20 units (0.2 mL) = 200 mcg OR 40 units (0.4 mL) = 400 mcg.
Dr. Lawrence's preference: 1% lidocaine with epinephrine 1:100,000. Draw 100 units (1 mL) in an insulin syringe for the MD.
⚠️ Important
  • Check BP and pulse immediately prior to injection and again 5 minutes after.
  • Inject medication very slowly (over ~1 minute).
  • If patient is hypertensive, consider a different treatment.
🧰 Treatment Supplies
  • Alcohol prep pads (4–6)
  • Phenylephrine 1% 10 mg/mL — 1 vial
  • 0.9% NaCl 10 mL — 1 vial
  • 1% lidocaine w/ epi 1:100,000 (Dr. Lawrence's preference)
  • 3 mL leur-lock syringe (1)
  • 10 mL leur-lock syringe (1)
  • 60 mL leur-lock syringe (1)
  • 18g × 1½″ needle (1)
  • 18g × 1.16″ angiocath (2)
  • 16g angiocath (1)
  • 16g butterfly needle (2)
  • 18g butterfly needle (2)
  • 100-unit insulin syringe (3)
➕ Additional Supplies
  • 1 L bottle normal saline irrigant (×2)
  • Large basin for irrigant/clots
  • Sterile gloves (per MD size)
  • Non-sterile gloves
  • Large chux
  • 4×4 gauze or kerlex fluff sponges
